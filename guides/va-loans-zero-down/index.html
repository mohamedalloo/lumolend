VA loans: the strongest play on the board — if you’ve earned it.

Zero down, no monthly mortgage insurance, and rates that routinely beat conventional. If you or your spouse served, this is almost always the first structure to price.

MA
Reviewed by Moh Alloo, Mortgage Loan Originator · NMLS #2732105 · West Capital Lending
Updated July 6, 2026

Why VA usually wins

  • 0% down — full financing without PMI of any kind.
  • No monthly mortgage insurance — the line item that quietly adds hundreds to low-down conventional and FHA payments simply does not exist.
  • Lower rates — the VA guaranty lets lenders price VA loans below comparable conventional, commonly by 0.25–0.5%.
  • Assumable — a qualified buyer can take over your rate later, a genuine resale asset in any higher-rate future.

Who is eligible

Broad strokes (the COE is the official answer): veterans meeting minimum active-duty service, current active-duty members after ~90 continuous days, National Guard and Reserve members (6 years, or shorter with qualifying active-duty periods), and surviving spouses of service members who died in the line of duty or from a service-connected disability.

The Certificate of Eligibility

The COE proves entitlement. Lenders can usually pull it electronically in minutes through the VA portal — you rarely need to chase paperwork yourself. This is a day-one task in any VA file because it also states your entitlement amount.

The funding fee

Instead of mortgage insurance, VA charges a one-time funding fee, financeable into the loan: roughly 2.15% for first use at zero down, 3.3% for subsequent zero-down use, less with a down payment — and waived entirely for veterans receiving service-connected disability compensation and for eligible surviving spouses. A 10%-disability-rated veteran on a $500,000 purchase saves over $10,000 at closing versus an identical non-exempt file.

Entitlement and using VA more than once

Entitlement is reusable. It restores when a VA loan is paid off and the home is sold, and with today’s "bonus entitlement" rules many veterans can even carry two VA loans at once (for example after a PCS move) subject to remaining entitlement math. Never assume a past VA loan disqualifies you — run the numbers.

About VA offers in competitive markets

Some listing agents still believe VA offers close slower or appraise harshly. It is largely outdated: VA appraisals carry the Tidewater process and timelines comparable to conventional, and a well-prepared VA file closes in a normal purchase window. The fix is a strong, specific pre-approval and a loan officer who calls the listing agent to walk through the file.

Frequently asked questions

Do VA loans really require nothing down?
Yes — 0% down within your entitlement, with no monthly mortgage insurance. You still pay normal closing costs, though the seller can contribute and the funding fee can be financed.
What credit score does a VA loan need?
The VA itself sets no minimum; lender overlays typically start around 580–620. Pricing improves with score like any loan, but VA is notably forgiving of thinner credit.
Is the funding fee ever waived?
Yes — veterans receiving service-connected disability compensation and eligible surviving spouses are exempt. That exemption is worth thousands and is checked automatically when your COE is pulled.
Can I use a VA loan more than once?
Yes. Entitlement restores when a prior VA loan is paid off and the property sold, and bonus entitlement lets many veterans hold two VA loans simultaneously, subject to remaining-entitlement math.
Can surviving spouses use VA benefits?
Unremarried surviving spouses of service members who died in the line of duty or from a service-connected disability are generally eligible, including the funding-fee exemption.
